The essay structure

1. Introduction

Define the question in relation to the film, introduce your argument, and suggest the main areas your essay will explore.

2. Choice of sequence and justification

Identify the sequence briefly and explain why it gives you enough depth to answer the question in detail.

3. Analysis paragraphs

Make a clear point, use precise evidence, analyse how the film is constructed, explain the effect, and link back to the question.

4. Conclusion

Bring your argument together, answer the question again, and end with a clear final judgement.

The simplest way to remember it

Introduce the argument. Choose the right sequence. Analyse in depth. End with a judgement.

What makes up a Film Studies introduction?

Your introduction should be brief. You want to maximise your time analysing, but if you are clever, you can pick up useful AO1 marks straight away by including relevant knowledge.

Job of the introduction: define the question in the context of your film, introduce your argument, and explain the main factors you are going to explore.

Start by defining the question in the context of your film

Come to the essay with an argument already in mind. Introduce the film and the ideas or contexts that matter most to the question.

Example

Amy (Kapadia, 2015) can be seen as a documentary that carefully shapes the spectator's response through its use of archival footage, sound and editing. Although it presents itself through real material from Amy Winehouse's life, the film is far from neutral, instead constructing a powerful emotional version of its subject.

Why is this good?

It introduces the film, names a clear line of argument, and begins to establish the concepts the essay will return to.

Then explain how you will answer the question

Show the examiner the main factors your essay will focus on. This starts to build the structure of the response before the analysis begins.

Example

In this essay I will explore how Kapadia uses selected archival material, layered sound and emotionally shaped editing to influence how the spectator understands Amy, particularly in sequences that turn private moments into a carefully constructed public narrative.

Why is this good?

It outlines the structure of the essay, keeps the answer focused, and means later paragraphs can keep tying back to the introduction.

Introduce the film. Define the key idea in the question. State your overall argument. Indicate the main areas you will analyse.

Choosing and justifying a sequence

We usually recommend focusing on one sequence. The scene must have enough depth to answer the question well, otherwise the essay will quickly become general.

Job of the sequence paragraph: identify the scene briefly, then justify why it is the right one for the question.

Identify the scene very briefly

Tell the examiner exactly which sequence you are focusing on, but do not retell the whole scene. Keep it tight.

Example

To answer this question, I am going to focus on the sequence in which Amy records Back to Black, where the documentary combines studio footage, handwritten lyrics and layered sound to shape the spectator's view of her.

Why is this good?

It tells the examiner you know the sequence well without wasting time on unnecessary description.

Justify why this is the right sequence

Explain why this moment gives you enough material to answer the question in detail. This should almost outline the next part of the essay.

Example

This sequence is particularly useful because it allows close analysis of editing, sound and archival construction. It also presents Amy as both intimate and performative, which makes it a strong sequence for discussing how Kapadia shapes the spectator's emotional response.

Why is this good?

It proves the sequence has enough depth, starts to structure the essay, and shows that your analysis will be selective rather than random.

Name the sequence. Keep the description brief. Explain why this scene is rich enough to answer the question in detail.

Writing analysis paragraphs

This is where most of the marks are won. Each paragraph should do more than spot techniques or retell events. It should make a clear point about how the film is constructed and why that matters in relation to the question.

Job of the analysis paragraph: make a point, support it with precise evidence, analyse how the film is constructed, explain the effect, and link back to the question.
1. Start with a clear point

Open with an argument, not just a technique. The first sentence should answer the question directly.

Example

Kapadia uses editing and sound in the Back to Black recording sequence to construct Amy as both intimate and tragic, encouraging the spectator to admire her talent while also recognising how carefully her image is being shaped.

2. Use precise evidence from the sequence

Select a specific moment. Do not retell the whole sequence.

Example

As Amy begins to sing, Kapadia combines archival studio footage with handwritten lyrics on screen, while the soundtrack shifts between the rawness of her voice in the room and the finished track.

3. Analyse how the film is constructed

This is the key move. Use subject terminology, but explain what the choices actually do.

Example

The layering of diegetic and non-diegetic sound blurs the line between a private performance moment and Amy's public image as a star. The superimposed lyrics stylise the material, making the sequence feel less like untouched documentary footage and more like a carefully shaped narrative moment.

4. Explain the effect and link back to the question

Push the paragraph one step further by explaining why this matters.

Example

As a result, the spectator is encouraged to feel close to Amy, while also recognising that this closeness has been carefully manufactured through editing and sound design. This makes the sequence especially useful in showing how the documentary constructs emotional response rather than simply recording reality.

Make a point. Use precise evidence. Analyse how it is constructed. Explain the effect. Link back to the question.

Writing a conclusion

Your conclusion should be brief, clear and purposeful. This is not the place for new examples. Instead, it should bring your argument together and end with a clear final judgement.

Job of the conclusion: answer the question again, draw together your main argument, and end with a judgement. Top students can also include a brief personal reflection on the question.

Return directly to the question

Example

Overall, Amy demonstrates that Kapadia's documentary style is highly effective in shaping the spectator's response, because the film carefully constructs intimacy and sympathy rather than simply presenting events objectively.

Why is this good?

It sounds confident, focused and immediately reminds the examiner of your central argument.

Draw together your main line of argument

Example

Through his manipulation of archival footage, editing and sound, Kapadia turns real events into a shaped and emotionally powerful narrative that encourages the spectator to see Amy as both extraordinary and vulnerable.

Why is this good?

It summarises the key methods and ideas from the essay without just repeating each paragraph in order.

End with a judgement

Example

For that reason, a spectatorship or ideological approach is especially useful when studying Amy, because it reveals not just what the documentary shows, but how Kapadia constructs meaning and controls emotional response.

Why is this good?

It gives the essay a proper ending and shows evaluation, which matters in stronger responses.

For top students: add a brief personal response

This should be a thought about the question, not a review of the film.

Example

What I find most convincing, after analysing Amy in this way, is that the film feels less like neutral observation and more like a carefully shaped emotional experience, which makes the spectator's response central to understanding it.

Answer the question again. Draw together your main argument. End with a clear judgement. For top students, add a brief personal reflection on the question.
